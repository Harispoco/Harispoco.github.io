Online Course Subscription Analysis

Introduction

This Power BI dashboard was developed to analyze subscription performance across an online learning platform with a data-driven approach. The analysis focuses on understanding user demand patterns by examining total subscriptions across multiple dimensions, including topic categories, technologies, instructors, difficulty levels, and individual course titles. The dataset contains more than 16 million subscriptions, enabling a robust descriptive analysis to identify dominant trends, distribution patterns, and potential business risks.

The objective of this project is to transform raw subscription data into actionable insights by applying data modeling, aggregation logic, and interactive visualization techniques. The dashboard enables stakeholders to quickly assess performance drivers and detect concentration patterns within the platform.

Online Course Subscription Analysis Dashboard

Analytical Findings

The analysis reveals a strong concentration of subscriptions within the Programming category, which contributes approximately 55 percent of total subscriptions. This indicates that more than half of platform demand is centered around programming-related content. From a data perspective, this distribution highlights category dominance and suggests limited diversification across other knowledge domains. Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning follow as secondary contributors, each accounting for roughly 10 percent of subscriptions, indicating emerging but not yet dominant demand segments.

Technology-level analysis further reinforces this concentration pattern. Python overwhelmingly leads all other technologies in total subscriptions. The treemap visualization shows a significant gap between Python and other technology tags, demonstrating that Python acts as the primary demand driver within the platform ecosystem. This suggests a high correlation between programming-focused categories and Python-based courses. The dominance of a single technology may indicate strong market alignment but also introduces dependency risk if user preferences shift over time.

Instructor-level aggregation uncovers a highly skewed distribution pattern consistent with the Pareto principle. One instructor contributes a disproportionately large share of total subscriptions compared to peers. This long-tail distribution suggests that platform performance is significantly influenced by a small number of high-performing instructors. From a data analysis standpoint, this pattern indicates performance inequality and signals the need for further investigation into instructor-level variables such as topic alignment, course difficulty, publishing frequency, or learner ratings.

At the course title level, introductory courses generate the highest subscription volumes. “Introduction to Python” significantly outperforms all other courses, followed by intermediate-level Python content. This pattern suggests that user acquisition primarily occurs at the beginner level, forming a top-heavy funnel structure. The data implies strong entry-level demand but does not directly confirm progression rates to advanced courses, indicating an opportunity for further cohort or retention analysis.

Conclusion & Strategic Summary

From an analytical perspective, the dataset demonstrates category and technology concentration, instructor performance imbalance, and strong beginner-level acquisition. These patterns suggest that while the platform exhibits clear product-market fit within programming education, it may face scalability risks if diversification strategies are not implemented.

For decision-makers, these insights can inform content prioritization strategies, instructor performance evaluation frameworks, and learning path optimization. Additional analysis such as cohort retention, conversion tracking between course levels, and subscription lifecycle modeling would further strengthen strategic recommendations.

Technical Implementation

This dashboard was built using Power BI with a structured data model that enables dynamic filtering across topic, technology, instructor, and difficulty dimensions. Aggregation measures were created to calculate total subscriptions and distribution percentages. Interactive slicers allow cross-dimensional analysis, enabling users to drill down into specific segments and detect performance variations. The visual design emphasizes clarity in distribution comparison through donut charts, treemaps, and ranked bar charts.
